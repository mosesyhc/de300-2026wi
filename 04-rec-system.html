Handling missing data in recommendation systems

Matrix completion

Matrix completion typically assumes some low-rank structure within the dataset.

Examples of usage:

  • recommendation system
  • image restoration
  • generative image

Alternating Least Squares

One particular algorithm that has garnered considerable interest is the Alternating Least Squares algorithm (Hastie et al. 2015).

Suppose we have a matrix \(R \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\) that contains user ratings for items. The matrix completion or factorziation problem typically solves for some matrices \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{k\times n}, Y \in \mathbb{R}^{k\times m}\) such that \(R \approx X^\mathsf{T} Y\), through the optimization problem

\[\min_{X, Y} \ell(X, Y) := \underbrace{\frac{1}{2} \|R - X^\mathsf{T} Y\|^2_F}_\text{recovery} + \underbrace{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\|X\|^2_F + \|Y\|^2_F)}_\text{regularization}.\]

Observation:

  • \(\ell(X, Y)\) is non-convex.
  • \(\ell(X, Y)\) is bi-convex, meaning that fixing either \(X\), or \(Y\) leads to a convex function.

The bi-convexity leads to an alternating algorithm (for a full \(R\)). But the missing locations do introduce nuances to the algorithm. See details in Theorem 3.1 of (Hastie et al. 2015).

Bidirectional Encoder Representations (BERT) (Devlin et al. 2019)

Modern methods replace explicit low-rank assumptions with contextual representation learning, especially through masked prediction and self-attention.

Instead of predicting a single entry or factorizing the matrix directly, consider a full row as a sequence:

\[\mathbf{r}_i = (r_{i1}, r_{i2}, \dots, r_{im})\]

with missing entries treated as masked tokens.

The question becomes:
how do we predict a missing entry using all other entries in the row?

Consider a contextual encoder (BERT):

\[(\mathbf{h}_{i1}, \dots, \mathbf{h}_{im})=\mathrm{BERT}_\theta(\mathbf{r}_i)\]

where each \(\mathbf{h}_{ij}\) depends on both left and right context.

Prediction is made from a contextual embedding, e.g.,:

\[\hat{R}_{ij} = W \mathbf{h}_{ij}\]

Training is performed via masked reconstruction (randomly drop data in training):

\[\min_\theta \underbrace{\sum_{(i,j)\in\Omega_{\text{mask}}}\ell\big(\hat{R}_{ij}, R_{ij}\big)}_{\text{predict masked entries}}\]

Compare with classical matrix completion:

\[\min_{X,Y}\underbrace{\tfrac12 \|R - X^\mathsf{T}Y\|_F^2}_{\text{low-rank structure}} + \underbrace{\tfrac{\lambda}{2}(\|X\|_F^2+\|Y\|_F^2)}_{\text{regularization}}\]

Low rank adaptation (Hu et al. 2022)

Modern methods consider “pre-trained” data and adaptation to previously trained models, especially in the large language model perspective.

Instead of viewing all data at once, consider the prediction of row \(i\) of the matrix \(R\):

\[ \mathbf{r}_i = W \underbrace{\mathbf{e}_i}_{\text{unit vec.}} \]

The question becomes, how do we learn the weight matrix \(W\)?

Suppose there is a pre-trained estimate \(W_0\), then matrix completion technique is used in estimating the residual:

\[ \min_{X, Y} \ell(X, Y; W_0) := \underbrace{\frac{1}{2} \|R - W_0 - X^\mathsf{T} Y\|^2_F}_\text{fit residual} + \underbrace{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\|X\|^2_F + \|Y\|^2_F)}_\text{regularization} \]

Compare with:

\[ \min_{X, Y} \ell(X, Y) := \underbrace{\frac{1}{2} \|R - X^\mathsf{T} Y\|^2_F}_\text{recovery} + \underbrace{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\|X\|^2_F + \|Y\|^2_F)}_\text{regularization}. \]

Devlin, Jacob, Ming-Wei Chang, Kenton Lee, and Kristina Toutanova. 2019. “Bert: Pre-Training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding.” In Proceedings of the 2019 Conference of the North American Chapter of the Association for Computational Linguistics: Human Language Technologies, Volume 1 (Long and Short Papers), 4171–86.
Hastie, Trevor, Rahul Mazumder, Jason D Lee, and Reza Zadeh. 2015. “Matrix Completion and Low-Rank SVD via Fast Alternating Least Squares.” The Journal of Machine Learning Research 16 (1): 3367–3402.
Hu, Edward J, Yelong Shen, Phillip Wallis, Zeyuan Allen-Zhu, Yuanzhi Li, Shean Wang, Lu Wang, Weizhu Chen, et al. 2022. “Lora: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models.” ICLR 1 (2): 3.
